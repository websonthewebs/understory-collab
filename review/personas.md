# Marketing Personas — Understory Collaborative

These personas represent the primary buyer archetypes for Understory Collaborative. Use them to evaluate all website content, messaging, and design decisions through the lens of real prospective clients.

---

## Persona 1: "Strategic Sara" — The VP of Digital Transformation

| Attribute | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Title** | VP of Digital Transformation / Chief Digital Officer |
| **Company** | Mid-market enterprise, 500–5,000 employees |
| **Industry** | Healthcare, Education, or Retail |
| **Age Range** | 38–50 |
| **Reports To** | CIO or CEO |
| **Budget Authority** | $250K–$2M annually for external partners |

### Goals
- Modernize legacy systems without disrupting operations
- Find a partner who understands her business, not just the technology
- Demonstrate measurable ROI to the C-suite within 6–12 months
- Reduce vendor fragmentation (wants strategy + implementation from one partner)

### Pain Points
- Has been burned by consultants who deliver slide decks but can't execute
- Tired of dev shops that build what's specced without questioning whether it's right
- Needs accessibility compliance but doesn't know where to start
- Internal teams are stretched thin; needs someone who can ramp up fast

### How She Finds Partners
- Peer referrals and industry conferences
- LinkedIn thought leadership content
- Google search for "[industry] + digital transformation consulting"
- Evaluates case studies and portfolio depth before scheduling a call

### What She Needs from the Website
- Proof of industry expertise (case studies with outcomes)
- Clear articulation of how advisory and implementation connect
- Low-friction path to a conversation (not a sales funnel)
- Signals of credibility: team experience, methodology, real results

### Key Objections
- "Are they big enough to handle our scale?"
- "Do they actually know our industry or is it generic?"
- "How is this different from the last three consultancies we hired?"

## Persona 2: "Technical Tom" — The CTO / VP of Engineering

| Attribute | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Title** | CTO / VP of Engineering / Head of Platform |
| **Company** | Growth-stage company or mid-market enterprise, 100–3,000 employees |
| **Industry** | EdTech, Transportation, Legal Tech |
| **Age Range** | 35–48 |
| **Reports To** | CEO or COO |
| **Budget Authority** | $100K–$1M for external development partnerships |

### Goals
- Augment his team's capacity without losing architectural control
- Find developers who write production-quality, maintainable code
- Get honest technical assessments — not just agreement with his existing plan
- Ship features faster while reducing technical debt

### Pain Points
- Has had bad experiences with offshore teams that require heavy management
- Skeptical of "full-service" firms — worries they're a mile wide, inch deep
- Needs partners who understand DevOps, CI/CD, and modern tooling
- Frustrated by consultants who can't read code or review PRs

### How He Finds Partners
- GitHub presence and open-source contributions
- Technical blog posts and architecture write-ups
- CTO peer networks and Slack communities
- Clutch, G2, or similar review platforms

### What He Needs from the Website
- Technical depth: specific stacks, methodologies, delivery practices
- Evidence of real engineering culture (not just marketing speak)
- Clear explanation of how they integrate with existing teams
- Fast path to a technical conversation, not a sales qualifier

### Key Objections
- "Will their developers actually be senior, or will I get juniors?"
- "Can they work within our existing architecture and processes?"
- "Are they going to try to upsell me on advisory when I just need builders?"

## Persona 3: "Operations Olivia" — The COO / Director of Operations

| Attribute | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Title** | COO / Director of Operations / Head of Process Improvement |
| **Company** | Mid-market company, 200–2,000 employees |
| **Industry** | Healthcare, Legal Services, Retail |
| **Age Range** | 40–55 |
| **Reports To** | CEO |
| **Budget Authority** | $150K–$800K for operational technology projects |

### Goals
- Automate manual processes that are consuming staff time
- Improve data visibility and reporting across the organization
- Ensure any new technology is adopted by her teams (change management)
- Meet compliance requirements (HIPAA, ADA, SOC 2) without slowing down

### Pain Points
- Technology projects have failed before because nobody managed the people side
- Doesn't speak developer jargon — needs a partner who communicates in business terms
- Overwhelmed by vendor options and doesn't know how to evaluate technical proposals
- Worried about disruption to daily operations during implementation

### How She Finds Partners
- Referrals from her professional network
- Industry association events and webinars
- Google search for "process automation consulting [industry]"
- Values firms that lead with business outcomes, not technology features

### What She Needs from the Website
- Language that connects technology to business outcomes
- Proof that the firm understands change management, not just code
- A clear, jargon-free explanation of the engagement process
- Testimonials or case studies from similar-sized organizations in her industry

### Key Objections
- "Will they actually listen to our people, or just impose a solution?"
- "How do I know this won't be another failed IT project?"
- "Can they help me make the case internally for this investment?"

## Persona 4: "Founder Frankie" — The Startup / Scale-up Founder

| Attribute | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Title** | CEO / Founder / Co-founder |
| **Company** | Seed to Series B startup, 10–100 employees |
| **Industry** | EdTech, Accessibility Tech, SaaS |
| **Age Range** | 28–45 |
| **Reports To** | Board of Directors / Investors |
| **Budget Authority** | $50K–$500K (often milestone-based or grant-funded) |

### Goals
- Build an MVP or v2 product with a team that moves fast
- Get strategic guidance on technical architecture decisions before committing
- Find a partner willing to work in phases as funding becomes available
- Build accessibility in from the start (not bolt it on later)

### Pain Points
- Can't afford to hire a full engineering team yet
- Previous freelancers or agencies delivered late, over budget, or abandoned the project
- Needs someone who can challenge product assumptions, not just take orders
- Investors are asking about scalability and technical due diligence

### How They Find Partners
- Startup ecosystem referrals (accelerators, investors, founder communities)
- Twitter/X and LinkedIn content
- Google search for "startup CTO-as-a-service" or "fractional CTO consulting"
- Portfolio and case study review

### What They Need from the Website
- Flexibility: phased engagements, not rigid enterprise contracts
- Evidence of startup experience and speed
- Proof of advisory capability (architecture, roadmapping) alongside build
- Accessibility expertise as a differentiator for their product

### Key Objections
- "Can they work within our budget constraints?"
- "Will they move at startup speed or slow us down with process?"
- "Are they going to push enterprise solutions we don't need yet?"

## Using These Personas

Each review group should reference these personas in their work:

| Review Group | How to Use Personas |
|---|---|
| **Marketing Review** | Evaluate whether messaging, CTAs, and content resonate with each persona's goals and objections |
| **UX Review** | Assess whether the information architecture serves each persona's navigation patterns and decision needs |
| **Client Critique** | Adopt a specific persona and walk through the site as that person, documenting friction points and gaps |
